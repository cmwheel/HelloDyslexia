Formal Evaluation

How to request a formal evaluation for dyslexia — through your school district or privately. Your child has rights, and so do you.

Evidence-based · California-specific guidance

Option 1

School-based evaluation.

Under federal law (IDEA), your school district must evaluate your child for free if you suspect a learning disability. You don’t need a doctor’s referral — just a written request.

1

Submit a written request

Write a letter to your school’s principal or special education department requesting a comprehensive evaluation. Send it via email and certified mail so you have a paper trail. The clock starts when the school receives your letter.

2

Receive the Assessment Plan

In California, the school must provide you with a written Assessment Plan within 15 calendar days of receiving your request. This plan describes what they’ll test and who will conduct the evaluation.

3

Sign the Assessment Plan

You have 15 days to review and sign (or decline). Once you sign, the 60-day evaluation window begins (California Ed Code §56043). The school must complete testing and hold an IEP meeting within that window.

4

IEP meeting & eligibility decision

A multidisciplinary team reviews the results and determines if your child qualifies for special education under “Specific Learning Disability.” If eligible, the team creates an IEP with specialized instruction and accommodations.

Good to know: If your child doesn’t qualify for an IEP, they may still be eligible for a 504 Plan providing classroom accommodations like extended time and audiobooks.

Template

Sample request letter.

Copy, customize, and send. Replace the bracketed sections with your information.

[Today’s Date] [Your Name] [Your Address] [Your Email] · [Your Phone] [Principal or Special Education Director] [School Name] [School Address] Re: Written Request for Special Education Evaluation — [Child’s Full Name], DOB [XX/XX/XXXX], Grade [X] Dear [Name], I am writing to formally request a comprehensive evaluation of my child, [Child’s Name], to determine eligibility for special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and California Education Code §56300 et seq. I have concerns that [Child’s Name] may have a specific learning disability, including possible dyslexia, affecting [his/her/their] ability to read, decode words, spell, and/or process phonological information. Specific concerns include: • [e.g., difficulty sounding out unfamiliar words] • [e.g., struggles with spelling despite significant effort] • [e.g., reading fluency significantly below grade level] I am requesting a full evaluation that includes assessment of phonological processing, phonological memory, rapid automatic naming, decoding skills, reading fluency, spelling, and any other areas relevant to determining the presence of a specific learning disability. I understand that under California law (Ed Code §56043), the district must provide me with a written Assessment Plan within 15 calendar days of this request, and must complete the evaluation within 60 calendar days of my signing that plan. Please confirm receipt of this letter and advise me of next steps. Sincerely, [Your Name]

Tip: Send via email and certified mail. Keep a date-stamped copy. If you don’t hear back within 15 days, follow up in writing citing Ed Code §56043.

Option 2

Private evaluation.

A private evaluation gives you a comprehensive, detailed picture — and an explicit dyslexia diagnosis if warranted. It’s an investment, but it can be worth it.

When it’s worth it

If you disagree with the school’s findings, need an explicit dyslexia diagnosis, or want detailed recommendations for tutoring and accommodations (including SAT/ACT).

Typical cost

Educational psychology evaluations run $1,500–$3,500 nationally. In California metro areas, expect $2,500–$5,000+. University training clinics may offer evaluations for $500–$1,500.

Who does them

Educational psychologists, neuropsychologists, or licensed clinical psychologists with learning disability expertise. Check credentials before hiring.

Good to know: Schools must consider private evaluation results, but aren’t required to implement them. If you disagree with the school’s evaluation, you can request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense.

What to Expect

During the evaluation.

A comprehensive dyslexia evaluation typically takes 4–8 hours across one or two sessions. Here’s what they’ll look at:

Phonological processing & decoding

How your child hears, remembers, and manipulates sounds in language. Includes reading real words and nonsense words (nonsense words can’t be memorized — a critical measure).

Reading fluency & rapid naming

How quickly and accurately your child reads words, sentences, and passages. Rapid naming speed is a key indicator of dyslexia.

Spelling & written expression

Spelling of isolated words and writing at the sentence and paragraph level.

Comprehension & vocabulary

Listening and reading comprehension, plus receptive vocabulary. Helps distinguish decoding difficulties from broader language challenges.

Know Your Rights

You have more power than you think.

  • You can request an evaluation in writing at any time — no referral needed
  • The school must respond with an Assessment Plan within 15 calendar days (California)
  • Evaluation must be completed within 60 calendar days of your signed consent
  • You have the right to results explained in understandable language
  • You can request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the school’s findings
  • Your child must be evaluated in their primary language
  • You have full due process rights: mediation, complaints, and hearings
  • If your child doesn’t qualify for an IEP, Section 504 may provide classroom accommodations
  • AB 1369 & SB 114 (2015): Established California’s first official dyslexia policy framework and screening guidelines
  • AB 1454 (2025): Requires universal K–2 screening for risk of dyslexia statewide
  • Ed Code §56043: The 15/15/60-day evaluation timeline is legally binding
  • If the school doesn’t respond within 15 days, follow up in writing citing the Education Code
