Understanding Your Screening Results

Pick your child’s screener below for a plain-English explanation of the results.

Based on current reading science

Select your screener

Which one did your school use?

mCLASS (DIBELS 8th Edition) is the most widely used reading screener in California. Brief one-on-one assessments with the teacher measure foundational skills.

What it measures

Letter naming, phonemic awareness, nonsense word fluency, and oral reading fluency. Each subtest targets a different skill.

What the scores mean

Results are “Above Benchmark,” “At Benchmark,” “Below Benchmark,” or “Well Below Benchmark.” Below or Well Below means your child may need additional support.

At risk vs. on track

“At risk” means the score suggests reading difficulty without intervention. It does not mean your child can’t learn to read — it means targeted instruction now will help.

What to do next

  1. Ask which subtests your child scored low on and what intervention is already in place.
  2. Request the full score report. You have the right to see every subtest score.
  3. If “Well Below Benchmark” on two or more subtests, ask about a formal evaluation.

Amira is an AI-powered reading assessment. Your child reads aloud to a device; the software analyzes fluency, accuracy, and comprehension in real time.

What it measures

Oral reading fluency, word recognition accuracy, and comprehension. Difficulty adapts based on your child’s responses.

What the scores mean

Students are categorized as “On Track,” “Needs Support,” or “Urgent.” Needs Support or Urgent means reading is below grade-level expectations.

At risk vs. on track

“Urgent” means the AI detected significant difficulty. Results can be affected by test anxiety, background noise, or comfort with the device.

What to do next

  1. Ask if Amira results match classroom performance. If not, request a re-do or another measure.
  2. Request which skill areas showed difficulty (fluency, accuracy, or comprehension).
  3. If “Urgent,” ask what intervention plan is in place and how progress is monitored.

ROAR (Stanford University) is a digital assessment that measures key reading skills with game-like tasks on a computer or tablet.

What it measures

Single-word reading (decoding), sentence reading efficiency, and phonological awareness. Timed tasks; students select words or identify sounds.

What the scores mean

Scores are percentiles vs. same-grade peers. Below the 25th percentile typically flags risk (e.g. 15th percentile = higher than 15% of peers, lower than 85%).

At risk vs. on track

Below 25th percentile = “at risk.” Your child may need more structured instruction. Comfort with technology can affect results.

What to do next

  1. Ask for percentile scores on each subtest, not just the risk category.
  2. Compare with classroom data. If they don’t match, ask for a follow-up assessment.
  3. Below 15th percentile is a strong signal to request a formal evaluation.

Multitudes is designed for culturally and linguistically diverse learners. It measures reading and language skills while accounting for home language.

What it measures

Phonological awareness, decoding, vocabulary, and comprehension. It helps distinguish reading difficulty from normal second-language learning.

What the scores mean

Skill levels: “Proficient,” “Developing,” or “Needs Intervention.” The platform considers your child’s language background.

At risk vs. on track

“Needs Intervention” means difficulty beyond what’s expected for language background. For multilingual families, some patterns are normal in bilingual development.

What to do next

  1. Ask whether the assessment was in your child’s primary language, English, or both.
  2. Request which skills were flagged. Phonological awareness issues often signal dyslexia across languages.
  3. If “Needs Intervention,” ask about support for both home language and English reading.

Strengths Spotlight

This screener measures one skill: decoding. It does not measure the creativity, problem-solving, or big-picture thinking that dyslexic minds often excel at.

A low score on a reading screener tells you about one narrow skill — it says nothing about your child’s intelligence, imagination, or potential.
