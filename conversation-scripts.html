Conversation Scripts

You don’t have to figure out what to say on the spot. These are the exact words other parents have used in school meetings — tested, collaborative, and ready for you to use today.

Collaborative tone · Schools as partners · Your rights, clearly explained

Before the meeting

How to use these scripts.

School meetings can feel overwhelming. These scripts give you a starting point — you don’t need to memorize them. Pick the scenario that matches your situation, read through the script once, and bring it with you (on your phone or printed out).

Bring documentation

Screening results, report cards, work samples, outside evaluations — anything that shows a pattern. Schools respond to data.

Bring a partner

If possible, bring your spouse, a family member, or an advocate. Two sets of ears are better than one. They can take notes while you talk.

Take notes

Write down names, dates, and what was agreed to. After the meeting, email a summary: “Per our conversation, here’s what we agreed…”

Stay collaborative

Most teachers want to help. Start with “we” language: “What can we do together?” You can be firm about your child’s rights and kind at the same time.

Choose a scenario

Find your situation.

Each script gives you an opening sentence, the most important questions to ask, what to listen for in the school’s response, and what to do if they push back.

Use this when: Your child has been flagged on a screener, or you have concerns about their reading, and you want the school to conduct a comprehensive evaluation to determine if they qualify for special education services or a 504 plan.

Your opening

Thank you for meeting with me. I’ve been observing some challenges with [child’s name]’s reading, and based on [the screening results / what I’m seeing at home], I’d like to formally request a comprehensive evaluation to understand their learning needs. I want to make sure we’re doing everything we can, and I know an evaluation is the best way to get a clear picture.

Adapt this to your situation. If you have screening data, reference it specifically.

Tone tip: Frame it as working together, not making a demand. “I want to understand” and “help us figure out” are powerful phrases that invite collaboration.

Questions to ask

  • What do I need to submit in writing to formally start the evaluation process? I want to make sure my request is properly documented.
  • What’s the timeline? How many days does the school have to respond to my request and complete the evaluation? (In most states, this is 60 days from consent.)
  • What types of assessments will be included? Will they specifically assess reading, decoding, phonological awareness, and written expression?
  • Can I share my own observations or outside evaluations to include in the review?
  • Who will be on the evaluation team, and when will I receive the results?

What to listen for

  • A clear, specific timeline — federal law requires evaluation within 60 days of consent in most states; some states have shorter timelines.
  • Confirmation that the evaluation will cover reading-specific areas, not just a general cognitive or academic assessment.
  • Your right to participate in the eligibility determination meeting and to receive copies of all reports.
  • If they suggest waiting instead: this is a yellow flag. You have the right to request an evaluation at any time, regardless of MTSS tier.

If they push back or decline

“I understand you may feel [child] needs more time. However, I’d like to exercise my right under IDEA to request a formal evaluation. If the school declines, I’m requesting the denial in writing, including the reasons. I’d also like information about my right to an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense.”

After the meeting

Follow up with a written letter or email confirming your request. Keep copies of everything. Note who was present, what was discussed, and any timelines given.

Use this when: The school has told you to “wait and see,” “give it more time,” or “let’s see how they do this year” before evaluating or providing additional support. Research consistently shows early intervention leads to the best outcomes.

Your opening

I appreciate your perspective, and I understand the idea of monitoring over time. That said, the research on reading difficulties is clear that early intervention — ideally by the end of first grade — leads to significantly better outcomes. I don’t want us to lose that window. I’d like to understand what we can do now, even while we’re monitoring.

This is one of the most common situations parents face. The key is to be respectful but persistent.

Key fact: Under federal law (IDEA), a parent’s request for evaluation cannot be delayed simply because a child is in RTI or MTSS. The school must respond to your written request, regardless of what tier your child is in.

Questions to ask

  • What specific data are you basing the recommendation to wait on? Can I see the progress monitoring data?
  • What interventions or support can start now while we monitor? I’d rather not wait without any action being taken.
  • What’s the specific timeline for this waiting period? When will we reconvene to review? I’d like a date, not “later this year.”
  • If my child doesn’t improve during this period, will an evaluation automatically be initiated, or will I need to request one again?
  • Can you share the research that supports waiting in a situation like my child’s?

What to listen for

  • Willingness to provide intervention now (Tier 2 or Tier 3 support), not just “more time in the general classroom.”
  • A concrete follow-up date — not “we’ll check back later” but “we’ll meet again on [date] to review data.”
  • Acknowledgment that you can request an evaluation at any time, regardless of MTSS progress.
  • If they refuse any interim support: this is a red flag. Support should start while decisions are being made, not after.

If they push back or still refuse

“I respect the team’s input, but I’d like to put my formal request for evaluation in writing today. Under IDEA, a parent’s request for evaluation cannot be delayed because a child is in MTSS or RTI. I’ll follow up with a written letter by [date]. Please provide any denial in writing, including the data supporting that decision.”

After the meeting

Send a written request for evaluation immediately — don’t wait. Start your own documentation at home: dates, what you’re observing, and what the school said. This creates a paper trail.

Use this when: Your child is in MTSS (Multi-Tiered System of Supports) and you want to understand exactly what support they’re getting, whether it’s working, and what would trigger a move to more intensive help or a formal evaluation.

Your opening

I’d like to get a clear picture of where [child’s name] is in the MTSS process right now. Can you walk me through the current tier of support, what the instruction looks like, and the data you’re using to track progress? I want to make sure the plan is working and that we have a clear path forward.

MTSS has 3 tiers. Tier 1 = general classroom. Tier 2 = small group. Tier 3 = intensive, often 1:1. Not sure what tier your child is in? That’s exactly what to ask.

What to know: If your child has been in Tier 2 for 6–8 weeks without improvement, that’s a signal to discuss Tier 3 or a formal evaluation. You don’t have to wait for the school to suggest it.

Questions to ask

  • What tier is my child currently in, and when did they move to this tier?
  • What specific intervention program is being used? How often, for how many minutes, and in what setting (small group, one-on-one)?
  • What progress monitoring data do you have? How often is it collected, and can I see the most recent results?
  • What would trigger a move to a higher tier or a referral for formal evaluation? What are the specific criteria?
  • Is the intervention evidence-based for reading difficulties specifically (e.g., structured literacy, Orton-Gillingham), or is it a general reading program?

What to listen for

  • A specific program name and schedule (e.g., “Wilson Reading, 30 minutes, 4x/week in a group of 4”). Vague answers like “extra reading time” aren’t enough.
  • Actual data showing the trend — is your child improving, flat, or declining? Ask to see the graph or data points.
  • Clear criteria for moving tiers, not vague language like “if things don’t improve.” You want specifics.
  • Commitment to share progress data with you on a regular schedule (every 2–4 weeks is standard).

If they push back

“I understand there’s a process, but I want to make sure the current intervention is the right fit. If my child isn’t making adequate progress after [6–8 weeks], I’d like to discuss moving to more intensive support or requesting a formal evaluation. Can we put that timeline and those criteria in writing?”

After the meeting

Ask for a copy of the intervention plan and the most recent progress monitoring data. Mark the follow-up date on your calendar. If you don’t hear back by then, email the teacher to check in.

Use this when: Your child has been identified with dyslexia (or reading difficulty that substantially limits their learning) and you want to request formal accommodations through a 504 plan. A 504 plan provides accommodations so your child can access the general curriculum on equal footing.

Your opening

Based on [the evaluation results / our child’s dyslexia identification / the screening data], I’d like to begin the process of requesting a 504 plan. I want to make sure [child’s name] has the accommodations they need to fully access the curriculum and show what they know, not just what they can decode.

504 plans are different from IEPs. A 504 provides accommodations (how your child accesses learning); an IEP provides specialized instruction. Your child might benefit from one or both.

Come prepared: Before the meeting, make a list of specific situations where your child struggles (timed tests, reading aloud, copying from the board, long writing assignments). Concrete examples are more persuasive than general concerns.

Questions to ask

  • Who is the 504 coordinator at this school, and what documentation do I need to submit to request an evaluation for 504 eligibility?
  • Can I submit outside evaluations, screening results, and teacher observations as part of the documentation?
  • What accommodations are commonly provided for students with dyslexia? I’d like to discuss: extended time, audiobooks / text-to-speech, note-taking support, alternative testing formats, and reduced copying requirements.
  • How often will the plan be reviewed, and how will we know if the accommodations are working?
  • What happens if we disagree on the accommodations included in the plan?

What to listen for

  • A clear process with a timeline for the eligibility determination meeting.
  • Willingness to consider a range of accommodations — not just “extra time on tests.” Dyslexia affects reading, writing, and testing differently.
  • Confirmation that accommodations will cover multiple areas: reading access, writing support, testing formats, and classroom participation.
  • Your right to be part of the 504 team meeting and to receive the plan in writing.

If they push back or deny eligibility

“I’d like to submit my request for a 504 evaluation in writing. Under Section 504, schools must evaluate a student who is believed to have a disability that substantially limits a major life activity — reading qualifies. If the school determines my child isn’t eligible, I’d like the decision and reasoning in writing, along with information about the grievance procedure.”

After the meeting

Submit your request in writing with supporting documentation. Prepare your own list of accommodation ideas to bring to the 504 plan meeting. Review the plan annually and request changes if accommodations aren’t being implemented.

Use this when: You want to know if your child’s reading instruction uses evidence-based methods specifically designed for students with dyslexia. Structured literacy (explicit, systematic, multisensory) is the gold standard for dyslexia intervention.

Your opening

I’ve been learning about structured literacy and how it’s recommended for students with dyslexia and reading difficulties. I’d like to understand what reading instruction [child’s name] is currently receiving — specifically, whether it follows a structured literacy approach, and who is delivering it.

You don’t need to be an expert on structured literacy to ask about it. These questions will help you understand what your child is getting and whether it matches best practices.

Programs to listen for: Wilson Reading System, Lindamood-Bell, Barton Reading, Orton-Gillingham, RAVE-O, SPIRE, and Fundations are all evidence-based. If the school says “balanced literacy” or “guided reading” alone, those are not structured literacy and are not recommended as the primary intervention for dyslexia.

Questions to ask

  • What program or method is being used for my child’s reading intervention? What’s it called specifically?
  • Is the instruction explicit, systematic, and multisensory? Does it follow a specific scope and sequence and use multiple senses (seeing, hearing, touching)?
  • Is the person delivering the instruction trained and certified in this specific method? What training have they completed?
  • How often does my child receive this instruction, and is it in addition to or instead of regular classroom reading time?
  • If the school doesn’t currently use structured literacy, are there plans to adopt it? What alternatives can we explore?

What to listen for

  • A specific program name (you can look it up later to verify it’s evidence-based for dyslexia).
  • Frequency and intensity: For students with significant reading difficulty, ideally 4–5x per week, 30–45 minutes per session. Less than 3x/week is usually insufficient.
  • Whether the instructor is trained and certified in the program — not just generally trained in reading instruction, but in the specific method being used.
  • If they mention “balanced literacy” or “guided reading” only: ask specifically how phonics, decoding, and phonological awareness are taught. These methods alone are not sufficient for students with dyslexia.

If they push back

“I understand the school has its current programs, but the research is strong that structured literacy approaches are the most effective for students with dyslexia. I’d like us to document this conversation and revisit in [timeframe]. In the meantime, can you share the evidence that supports the current approach for students with my child’s reading profile?”

After the meeting

Research the named program to verify it’s evidence-based. If it’s not, consider requesting different instruction or supplementing with outside tutoring from a certified practitioner. Many families combine school support with private structured literacy tutoring.

Use this when: You’ve received your child’s reading screening results (from SB 114 or your school’s universal screening program) and want to understand what the scores mean, whether your child needs support, and what happens next.

Your opening

Thank you for sharing [child’s name]’s screening results. I want to make sure I understand what these scores mean, where my child stands compared to grade-level expectations, and what the school’s plan is going forward. Can we go through the results together?

It’s OK to not understand the scores yet. That’s what this conversation is for. Bring the results letter with you.

Don’t be afraid of jargon: If they use terms like NWF, ORF, PSF, or “composite score,” ask them to explain in plain language. Use our Jargon Translator as a reference.

Questions to ask

  • Can you walk me through each subtest? What was measured, what my child’s score was, and what it means in plain language?
  • Which areas are the biggest concern, and which are relative strengths? What does the overall pattern suggest?
  • Based on these results, what is the school planning to do? Will there be additional intervention, further assessment, or a formal evaluation?
  • If my child scored “at risk,” what does that specifically trigger at this school? Does it automatically start support services?
  • When will my child be screened again, and what progress do you expect to see by then?

What to listen for

  • Clear, jargon-free explanation of what each score means. If you don’t understand, ask again. You have every right to understand your child’s data.
  • Whether “at risk” scores are triggering action or just being “monitored.” Monitoring without intervention is a missed opportunity.
  • A concrete next-step plan — not just “we’ll keep an eye on it.” You want: what intervention, how often, starting when, and when you’ll hear an update.
  • Your right to a copy of the full screening report. If they don’t offer it, ask for it in writing.

If they push back or minimize the results

“I appreciate the explanation. I’d like a copy of the full screening report for my records. If the results suggest my child may need additional support or evaluation, I’d like to put that request in writing today. The screening was designed to identify children who need more support, and I want to make sure we follow through on what the data is telling us.”

After the meeting

Get a copy of the full results. Use our Screener Guide to look up your child’s specific screener and understand the scores in depth. If your child scored “at risk” on two or more subtests, seriously consider requesting a formal evaluation.

Remember

These conversations aren’t just about reading challenges. Your child also has real cognitive strengths — creativity, big-picture thinking, problem-solving, empathy. The goal of every meeting is to get the right reading support so those strengths can shine. You’re not asking the school to “fix” your child. You’re asking them to support the whole child.
