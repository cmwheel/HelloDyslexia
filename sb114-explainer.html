SB 114 Explainer

California’s dyslexia screening law in plain language. SB 114 is one way dyslexia comes to light — 40+ states now have similar screening laws.

FAQ

Common questions about SB 114.

SB 114 is a California law that requires public schools to screen students in kindergarten and grades 1 and 2 for risk of dyslexia and other reading difficulties. The screening is universal (all students in those grades), and schools use one of several state-approved screeners. The goal is to identify children early so they can get support before they fall behind.
California law does not provide a broad opt-out for universal screening. If you have specific concerns (e.g., language, disability, or religious reasons), you can discuss them with your school. Policies may vary by district. For the most accurate answer for your situation, contact your school or district and, if needed, an education advocate or attorney.
If your child is flagged as “at risk” or “some risk,” the school should notify you and typically provide additional reading instruction (often through MTSS — Multi-Tiered System of Supports). Screening is not a diagnosis. You can request a copy of the results, ask what intervention is being offered, and, if appropriate, request a formal evaluation for special education or a 504 plan. Our Screener Guide and School Pathways guide explain next steps in detail.
Schools screen at designated times during the year (e.g., beginning, middle, end). Exact windows vary by district and screener. Your school or district can tell you when your child was or will be screened and when you’ll receive results.
You have the right to request your child’s screening results in writing. Contact the teacher, principal, or district office. Ask for the full score report (all subtests), not just a summary. Our Screener Guide helps you interpret the results once you have them.
No. More than 40 states now have laws requiring early reading screening. California’s SB 114 is part of a national trend toward identifying reading risk early so children can get help. If you’re in another state, check your state education department or Decoding Dyslexia chapter for your local requirements.
