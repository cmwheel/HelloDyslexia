Understanding the Result

What does “flagged” mean?

Screeners are early check-ins. They’re designed to identify students who may need extra support. Here’s what this result means — and what to do next.

Most schools use color systems like yellow or red.

Yellow usually means

  • Below benchmark
  • Extra support may be recommended
  • Progress should be monitored

Red usually means

  • Significantly below benchmark
  • Targeted intervention is needed
  • Follow-up should happen soon

A screener signals attention — not a label.

Possible Outcomes

What could this mean?

Some children:

Catch up quickly

With short-term, targeted support many children close the gap and read on level.

Need structured intervention

Some children benefit from a more structured, evidence-based reading intervention program.

May have a reading difference

An underlying reading difference such as dyslexia may be present and worth exploring further.

Right now, the focus is simple: make sure your child gets the right support early.

Take Action

What to do next.

1

Ask the teacher which specific skills were below benchmark

Was it phonemic awareness? Decoding? Fluency? Knowing which skills were flagged helps you understand exactly where your child needs support.

2

Ask what intervention will be provided

How often will your child receive support? In what group size? Using what program? These details matter.

3

Ask how progress will be measured

You should know what data the school will use to track whether the intervention is working.

4

Put a check-in date on your calendar

Plan to revisit progress in 6–8 weeks. This gives the intervention time to work and gives you a concrete moment to reassess.

Early understanding and action leads to better outcomes.

Worth Watching

When to look deeper.

It may be time to take a closer look if you’re noticing any of the following:

Dyslexia runs in the family

Siblings, parents, or close relatives with reading difficulties or a dyslexia diagnosis.

Sound-to-letter skills are hard

Trouble hearing sounds in words or matching sounds to letters (phonemic awareness).

Reading takes a lot of effort

It’s slow, tiring, or your child avoids reading whenever possible.

Support isn’t moving the needle

Limited progress after 6–8 weeks of consistent intervention may signal something deeper.
