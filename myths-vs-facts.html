Myths vs. Facts

The most common misconceptions about dyslexia, corrected. Share one card at a time with family, teachers, or friends.

What you might have heard.

Myth Fact

“Dyslexia means seeing letters or words backwards.”

Dyslexia is not a vision problem. It’s a difference in how the brain processes language sounds and links them to print. Reversing letters is common in early writers with or without dyslexia; it’s not the defining sign.

International Dyslexia Association

Myth Fact

“People with dyslexia are less intelligent.”

Dyslexia has nothing to do with intelligence. It affects how the brain decodes written language, not reasoning, creativity, or problem-solving. Many people with dyslexia have high IQs and excel in STEM, arts, and leadership.

IDA – Dyslexia and the Brain

Myth Fact

“Kids grow out of dyslexia.”

Dyslexia is lifelong. With the right instruction (e.g., structured literacy), reading and spelling can improve dramatically, but the underlying processing difference remains. Early intervention leads to the best outcomes.

IDA – Dyslexia at a Glance

Myth Fact

“They’re just not trying hard enough.”

Children with dyslexia often work harder than their peers to decode text. What looks like laziness or inattention is usually exhaustion or avoidance of a task that is genuinely difficult for their brain. Effort and outcome don’t match.

Based on reading science and neuroimaging research.

Myth Fact

“Dyslexia is rare.”

Up to 1 in 5 people have dyslexia — it’s the most common learning difference. It occurs across languages, cultures, and levels of instruction. Many go undiagnosed.

IDA – Dyslexia at a Glance

Myth Fact

“Dyslexia mostly affects boys.”

Dyslexia affects both boys and girls at similar rates. Boys are more often referred for evaluation because of behavioral or attention differences, so the gap you see in diagnosis is partly a referral bias, not prevalence.

Research on referral and identification rates (e.g., Quinn & Wagner, 2015).

Myth Fact

“Dyslexia only affects reading.”

Dyslexia primarily affects decoding and spelling, but it can also affect rapid naming, working memory, and organization. Many people with dyslexia have strengths in big-picture thinking, creativity, and spatial reasoning.

Made By Dyslexia – strengths-based research.

Myth Fact

“It’s too late to help after elementary school.”

It’s never too late. Older students and adults can still benefit from structured literacy and accommodations. Many discover their dyslexia as teens or adults and go on to thrive with the right support and strategies.

IDA and research on intervention across the lifespan.
