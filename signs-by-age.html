Signs of Dyslexia by Age

Scannable in 30 seconds. Pick your child’s age band to see both what to watch for and the strengths that often go with it.

Evidence-based · Free

Choose an age band

Reading & language signs

  • Late to talk or mix up sounds in words
  • Trouble learning nursery rhymes or rhyming games
  • Difficulty learning letter names or sounds
  • Family history of dyslexia or reading difficulty

Strengths you might notice

  • Strong verbal storytelling and imagination
  • Curiosity and big-picture thinking
  • Spatial play (building, puzzles, drawing)
  • Good at understanding when read to
Strengths at this age

Early signs of dyslexia are about how the brain processes sounds and symbols. They don’t predict your child’s creativity, problem-solving, or future success. Many dyslexic kids shine in hands-on and verbal learning.

Reading & language signs

  • Struggles with rhyming or recognizing words
  • Trouble learning the alphabet or letter sounds
  • Difficulty blending sounds into words
  • Slow to remember common words (the, is, and)
  • Avoids reading or gets frustrated when reading

Strengths you might notice

  • Vivid imagination and storytelling
  • Strong reasoning when discussing ideas
  • Good at building, designing, or visual tasks
  • Remembers stories and experiences in detail
Strengths at this age

K–2 is when many children are first flagged. The screener measures decoding. It does not measure your child’s creativity, persistence, or ability to think in pictures and stories. Those strengths are real and valuable.

Reading & writing signs

  • Reads slowly and with much effort
  • Skips or guesses words; difficulty with multisyllabic words
  • Spelling doesn’t match verbal ability
  • Trouble summarizing or remembering what was read
  • Avoids reading for pleasure

Strengths you might notice

  • Strong oral vocabulary and discussion
  • Creative and original ideas
  • Good at seeing patterns and connections
  • Excels in math reasoning, science, or art
Strengths at this age

By grades 3–5, the gap between decoding and comprehension can be obvious. So can the strengths: many dyslexic kids are the ones who ask the best questions, tell the best stories, or design the best projects.

Reading & writing signs

  • Reads below grade level; slow, labored reading
  • Spelling and writing lag behind ideas
  • Difficulty with note-taking and timed tests
  • May have been missed earlier; compensates with memory

Strengths you might notice

  • Big-picture and systems thinking
  • Strong narrative and verbal skills
  • Creativity, innovation, and problem-solving
  • Resilience and persistence
Strengths at this age

In middle and high school, dyslexic thinkers often excel in discussion, projects, and real-world tasks. The goal isn’t just to improve reading — it’s to support reading while unlocking these strengths.
