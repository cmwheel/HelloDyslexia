Your Child Was Flagged.

Take a breath. A screening flag is not a diagnosis. It’s the first useful piece of information you’ve gotten, and you’re in the right place.

Learn What It Means →
Evidence-based · Free · No login required

Step 1

What just happened.

Your child’s school administered a universal reading screener — a short test designed to identify students who may need extra reading support. Your child’s score fell into the “at risk” or “some risk” range.

A screener is a snapshot, not a verdict

Think of it like a vision screening at the pediatrician’s office. It doesn’t diagnose anything — it tells you that a closer look is worthwhile. Most children flagged by screeners benefit from targeted reading instruction, and many go on to read at or above grade level with the right support.

Step 2

What it means — and what it doesn’t.

A flag is a data point. It opens doors; it doesn’t close them.

What the flag IS

A data point that says your child may benefit from extra reading support
An opportunity to get targeted help early, when it matters most
The starting point for a conversation with your child’s school

What the flag is NOT

A diagnosis of dyslexia or any learning disability
A reflection of your child’s intelligence, effort, or potential
A reason to panic — children who get early support thrive

Step 3

What to do this week.

You don’t need to do everything at once. Start here.

1

Read your child’s screening results

Ask the school which screener was used and request a copy of the results. Our Screener Guide explains exactly what each score means in plain English.

2

Schedule a meeting with your child’s teacher

Ask: “What support is my child currently receiving? What are the next steps the school recommends?” You have the right to ask these questions.

3

Learn about your options

Depending on your child’s needs, the school may offer MTSS support, a 504 plan, or an evaluation for an IEP. Our School Pathways Guide (coming soon) breaks down each option.

4

Talk to your child with confidence

Let them know that their brain works differently — and that’s a strength. Many of the world’s most creative thinkers, entrepreneurs, and scientists are dyslexic.

5

Come back to Hello Dyslexia

New guides and tools are being added regularly. Bookmark this page and check back as you move through each stage of the journey.

Strengths Spotlight

A screening flag tells you one thing about reading. It says nothing about your child’s creativity, problem-solving, or potential. Dyslexic minds are wired for big-picture thinking, spatial reasoning, and making connections others miss. The path forward isn’t just about fixing reading — it’s about building on the strengths your child already has.
